History of AI

The Beginnings of Artificial Intelligence

Artificial intelligence, at its core, aims to imitate human intelligence by way of machines systems. The beginnings of artificial intelligence can be traced back on 1956 in the Darthmouth Summer Research Project in new Hampshire. Researchers from fields of language simulation, neuron nets, complexity theory and more discussed the idea of "thinking machines". The birth of artificial intelligence has affected multiple fields like engineering, mathematics, computer science, and alike.

Artificial Intelligence - The Golden Years

In the 1960s, the innovation in the field of AI grew rapidly. The creation of new programming languages, robots and automatons became popular. Algorithms that can solve mathematical problems were created. Computer scientist and professor, Joseph Weizenbaum, developed the very first chatbox in 1966 named ELIZA. ELIZA is an interactive computer program that could functionally converse in English with a person. Weizenbaum aimed to demonstrate that it is possible for the human mind to communicate with an artificially intelligent mind.

Artificial Intelligence - A disruptive technology

Disruptive technology is the technology that affects the normal operation of a market or an industry. It displaces a well-established product or technology, creating a new industry or market. In businesses theory, disruptive technology follows the tradition of identifying radical technical change and the creation of tools for its use at a firm or even policy level. As opposed to sustainable technologies, disruptive technologies, affects the way businesses, consumers, and even industries function. Artificial intelligence is a way of simulating the human mind. With electronic capacity and functionality increasing at an exponential rate, the capability of artificial intelligence extends from the simple automation to complex tasks. Its influence has extended to several aspects of human life and which businesses has taken advantage of.

Artificial Intelligence - In industries, businesses, and life

The power of artificial intelligence lies in its speed of execution, power of automation, ability to use complex equations, and accuracy of analysis. In industries, multiple tasks can be done by machines which are controlled by artificial intelligence; increasing production while decreasing cost. In businesses, artificial intelligence is used as predictive models in order to make future decisions. They are also used more intricately when dealing with the consumers of their product by using chatbots or suggesting products based on market trend. In everyday life, artificial intelligence is present in daily activities, such as social media and in simply searching the internet. The platforms you use recommend links or posts based on gathered data and trend.

Artificial Intelligence - In Healthcare

Artificial intelligence is progressively being used in healthcare. AI Technologies have different roles and tasks that fully support a specific healthcare field. Examples are machine learning, natural language processing, rule-based expert systems, physical robots, and robotic process automation. These technologies can revolutionize areas of medicine such as better organized healthcare logistics, mining medical records, diagnosis and treatment plans, administrative applications, drug creation, etc. Even though AI technologies are garnering attention in the medical field, real-life implementation is still a challenge, and the restrictions are the first key barrier. There are currently no standards to examine the safety and efficacy of AI systems under existing regulations. It is also evident that AI systems will not easily replace the roles of clinicians but will suffice the care for patients. Watch this video of the Medical Futurist speech on AI.

Timeline of Artificial Intelligence ( AIArtists.org )

Thinking in Numbers (1973)

Bayesian inference is a significant aspect of machine learning, and it is one of the earliest milestones in the history of artificial intelligence.

World War 2 triggers fresh thinking (1942)

Scientists from diverse disciplines, including the newly emerging sciences of neuroscience and computing, came together during World War Two. Mathematician Alan Turing and Neurologist Grey Walter talked about the "challenges of intelligent machines." Walter was also one of the people to build a robot. Turing created the Turing Test, which established the threshold for an intelligent machine: a computer that could mislead someone into thinking they were communicating with another human.

Unimate (1942)

Unimate, a 1950s-era industrial robot created by George Devol, was the first to work on a GM assembly line in New Jersey. Its duties included moving die castings from the assembly line and welding the parts onto cars.

WABOT-2 (1980)

Waseda University built WABOT-2. The WABOT's creation enabled the humanoid to speak with people, read musical scores, and perform music on an electronic keyboard.

A solution for big business (1987)

At the Digital Equipment Corporation, the first successful commercial expert system, known as the RI, began assisting configure orders for new computer systems. By 1986, the corporation had saved an estimated $40 million per year.

Man vs. machine: fight of the 20th century (1997)

Top-down AI supporters still had their champions in the form of supercomputers like Deep Blue, which defeated global chess champion, Garry Kasparov in 1997. The supercomputer won the contest, dubbed as "the brain's last stand," with such finesse that Kasparov assumed a human was in charge. This event was anticipated by some as the coming of age of AI. Others, though, saw this as just sheer force at work on a highly specialized task with well-defined criteria.

The first robot for the home (2002)

Rodney Brook's spin-off firm, iRobot, developed the first commercially successful home robot, the Roomba autonomous vacuum cleaner. Despite its basic sensors and low processing power, the device possessed enough intelligence to clean a house reliably and effectively. Roomba marked the beginning of a new era of autonomous robots dedicated to specialized duties.

Starting to crack the big problems (2008)

It appeared to be basic. This, however, signaled a tremendous advance. Although speech recognition is one of AI's main aims, decades of investment have never raised its accuracy above 80%. Google pioneered a new approach: huge numbers of powerful computers running parallel neural networks to learn to find patterns in massive amounts of data coming in from Google's many users.

Man vs machine: fight of the 21st century (2011)

"In 2011, IBM's Watson took on the human brain on US quiz show Jeopardy."
Watson was tasked with deciphering riddles and answering difficult queries. Its developers employed a variety of AI approaches, including neural networks, to teach the system to recognize patterns in questions and replies over three years. Watson thrashed its opponents, who were the show's two top performers of all time. The victory went viral and was hailed as a success for artificial intelligence.

Humanoid Robot named Sophia (2016)

Hanson Robotics has developed Sophia, a humanoid robot. Sophia is the first "robot citizen," and what sets her apart from prior humanoids is her resemblance to a real person, as seen by her capacity to see (image recognition), produce facial emotions, and speak with AI.

Bixby Says Hello (2018)

Bixby, a virtual assistant from Samsung, was launched. The virtual assistant's features include Voice: user can ask and speak recommendations; Vision: Bixby's uses its seeing ability to see what the users see; Home: Bixby uses app-based data to better perceive and connect with the user.
